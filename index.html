MethaneSAT

ABOUT

MethaneSAT is a joint mission between the United States and New Zealand that is expected to launch in 2022. It will discover and measure methane emissions from oil and gas activities virtually anywhere on Earth, producing quantitative data that will allow corporations and nations to identify, control, and reduce their methane emissions, thereby lowering the rate at which our world is warming. MethaneSAT LLC is a wholly-owned subsidiary of the Environmental Defense Fund. The Environmental Defense Fund and the New Zealand Space Agency will operate it.

HOW IT WORKS

Shortwave infrared wavelengths, where methane absorbs light, will be measured by a spectrometer on MethaneSAT's spectrometer for high precision. Using this method, we can detect variations in methane concentrations of 2 parts per billion (ppb). MethaneSAT's small pixel size (1-2 km2) and wide view path (200 km2) will be able to measure minor emission sources across huge areas that, in the aggregate, are important sources of methane emissions, but have been undetectable by satellites. The smaller the emission, the more precise the location can be determined with MethaneSAT's finer resolution data (although with less precision).

CHALLENGES OF METHANESAT

Due to Earth's surface light reflection, measurements on methane concentration variations are severely constrained in terms of quantity and precision. Cloud cover, high latitudes in the winter, and dark surfaces all reduce the quantity of light that the satellite sensor can pick up on.

All optical and infrared remote sensing systems are affected by clouds. MethaneSAT's mission planning algorithms include a way to predict where clouds will be, so that when possible, the satellite will go where there are no clouds.

MethaneSAT won't take measurements over high latitudes like Siberia during the darkest part of winter, when there isn't enough light to get useful information.

Most of Earth's land mass is reflective enough for imaging. MethaneSAT uses an alternate measurement that focuses the sensor to the bright glint location over seas where solar radiation is directly reflected off the Earth's surface (glint mode).
